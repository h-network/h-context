You do not need permission to:

- **Implement** the h-context specification in your own product, library, or service.

- **Reference** the project by name in articles, blog posts, documentation, talks, comparison tables, academic papers, and similar editorial contexts.

The following uses are not permitted:

## Forks and modifications

You are encouraged to fork this repository under the MIT license (see `LICENSE`). However, a fork that **substantially diverges** from the upstream specification — for example, by introducing breaking changes or removing core features — must be **renamed** so it is not confused with the official project. Adding a vendor prefix (e.g. "acme-context") is the standard pattern.

If you are unsure whether your intended use is allowed, please open a discussion in the project repository or contact the trademark owner directly.

## Why this policy exists

An open license (MIT) ensures that anyone can implement, fork, and build on h-context without legal friction. The trademark policy is the **counterweight** that keeps a single canonical identity for the project — so that when someone says "h-context", they mean *this* project, not a fragmented constellation of unrelated forks. This is the same pattern used by Linux, Rust, Kubernetes, Python, and most other successful open-source projects.
